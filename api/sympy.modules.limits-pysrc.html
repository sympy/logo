  1  """ 
  2  Limits 
  3  ====== 
  4   
  5  Implemented according to the PhD thesis 
  6  http://www.cybertester.com/data/gruntz.pdf, which contains very thorough 
  7  descriptions of the algorithm including many examples.  We summarize here the 
  8  gist of it. 
  9   
 10   
 11  All functions are sorted according to how rapidly varying they are at infinity 
 12  using the following rules. Any two functions f and g can be compared using the 
 13  properties of L: 
 14   
 15  L=lim  log|f(x)| / log|g(x)|           (for x -> infty)  
 16   
 17  We define >, < ~ according to:: 
 18       
 19      1. f > g .... L=+-infty  
 20       
 21          - f is greater than any power of g 
 22          - f is more rapidly varying than g 
 23          - f goes to infinity/zero faster than g 
 24       
 25       
 26      2. f < g .... L=0  
 27       
 28          - f is lower than any power of g 
 29       
 30      3. f ~ g .... L!=0,+-infty  
 31       
 32          - both f and g are bounded from above and below by suitable integral powers 
 33          of the other 
 34   
 35   
 36  Examples 
 37  ======== 
 38  :: 
 39      1 < x < exp(x) < exp(x^2) < exp(exp(x)) 
 40      1 ~ 3 ~ -5 
 41      x ~ x^2 ~ x^3 ~ 1/x ~ x^m ~ -x 
 42      exp(x) ~ exp(-x) ~ exp(2x) ~ exp(x)^2 ~ exp(x+exp(-x)) 
 43      f ~ 1/f 
 44   
 45  So we can divide all the functions into comparability classes (x and x^2 is the 
 46  same class, exp(x) and exp(-x) is some other class). In principle, we could 
 47  compare any two functions, but in our algorithm, we don't compare anything 
 48  below f=1 (for example log(x) is below 1), so we set f=1 as the lowest 
 49  comparability class.  
 50   
 51  Given the function f, we find the list of most rapidly varying (mrv set) 
 52  subexpressions of it. This list belongs to the same comparability class. Let's 
 53  say it is {exp(x), exp(2x)}. Using the rule f ~ 1/f we find an element "w" 
 54  (either from the list or a new one) from the same comparability class which 
 55  goes to zero at infinity. In our example we set w=exp(-x) (but we could also 
 56  set w=exp(-2x) or w=exp(-3x) ...). We rewrite the mrv set using w, in our case 
 57  {1/w,1/w^2}, and substitute it into f. Then we expand f into a series in w:: 
 58   
 59      f = c0*w^e0 + c1*w^e1 + ... + O(w^en),        where e0<e1<...<en, c0!=0 
 60   
 61  but for x->infty, lim f = lim c0*w^e0, because all the other terms go to zero, 
 62  because w goes to zero faster than the ci and ei. So:: 
 63   
 64      for e0>0, lim f = 0 
 65      for e0<0, lim f = +-infty   (the sign depends on the sign of c0) 
 66      for e0=0, lim f = lim c0 
 67   
 68  We need to recursively compute limits at several places of the algorithm, but 
 69  as is shown in the PhD thesis, it always finishes. 
 70   
 71  Important functions from the implementation: 
 72   
 73  compare(a,b,x) compares "a" and "b" by computing the limit L. 
 74  mrv(e,x) returns the list of most rapidly varying (mrv) subexpressions of "e" 
 75  rewrite(e,Omega,x,wsym) rewrites "e" in terms of w 
 76  leadterm(f,x) returns the lowest power term in the series of f 
 77  mrvleadterm(e,x) returns the lead term (c0,e0) for e 
 78  limitinf(e,x) computes lim e  (for x->infty) 
 79  limit(e,z,z0) computes any limit by converting it to the case x->infty 
 80   
 81  all the functions are really simple and straightforward except rewrite(), 
 82  which is the most difficult part of the algorithm.
